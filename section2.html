Phonological Processes



Russians and Crimean Tatars speak two mutually unintelligible languages originating from two different families of phonological contrast. The Russian language is of the Slavic family, which boasts a complex syllabic structure and can tolerate four-consonant clusters in both onsets and codas. For example, взгляд [vzglʲad] ‘glance’ has a four-consonant onset [vzglʲ]. In contrast, Crimean Tatar, a member of the Turkic family, allows no more than one consonant in syllabic onsets (Kavitskaya, 2010, pp. 29). As for codas, Crimean Tatar only permits two-consonant clusters if those clusters are of falling sonority, like in qart ‘old man’. In the context of Russian dominance in Crimea, this linguistic difference prompts an inevitable question: did the Crimean Tatar language have to adapt to the massive, violence-driven influx of Russian words with complex clusters, and if so, how?

1. Epenthesis

The distribution of sounds in every language is determined by a system of phonological rules known as "phonotactics". When these rules are violated, there are multiple ways for the language itself to adapt. For clusters, the most common method is epenthesis, wherein a vowel is added before or within an unlawful consonant cluster to break it up (Hall, 2011). For example, Spanish does not allow /st/ as an onset cluster, so when words like Middle French stupide or Latin status enter Spanish, they become estúpido and estado, with an <e> vowel added to the beginning so that the <s> will go with the <e> as a separate syllable. In another example, Japanese permits no more than one consonant in onset positions, so an English loanword like Christmas is realized as Kurisumasu (クリスマス), with the /kɹ/ cluster at the beginning of “Christmas” broken apart by inserting an <u> vowel.

In Crimean Tatar, many words that are borrowed from Russian do exhibit epenthesis. The following data set of Crimean Tatar words written in IPA is taken from Kavitskaya (2010):

(1) Crimean Tatar Russian
ɯʃqaf ʃkaf ‘wardrobe’
moskova moskva ‘Moscow’
qɯravat krovatʲ ‘bed’
vɯratʃ vratʃ ‘doctor’
pirizident prʲezʲidʲent ‘president’
pirsedatel prʲedsʲedatʲelʲ ‘chairman’

For the Russian word [ʃkaf], Crimean Tatar adds the high vowel [ɯ] before the voiceless postalveolar fricative [ʃ] to make the word [ɯʃqaf], thus allowing it to be projected as the disyllabic [ɯʃ.qaf.] rather than a monosyllable with a complex consonant onset. This is exactly the process behind the Spanish estúpido and estado that we have previously discussed. In another instance, the vowel [o] is inserted into the [kv] cluster in the name of the Russian capital, thus turning [moskva] into [moskova] in the same manner that Christmas is turned into Kurisumasu in Japanese. In other words, the data from Table 1 shows that Crimean Tatar possesses the same types of epenthesis that we have seen in other, more extensively studied languages.

2. Cluster simplification

Another way for a language to get rid of illicit clusters is via simplification. For example, the phonotactics of native Finnish words do not permit consonant cluster onsets; thus, the language simplified many of the cluster onsets in loanwords into the very last consonant of that cluster. This is how Swedish Franska ‘French’ became Finnish Ranska and Old Russian kristĭ became Finnish risti (Campbell, 2021, pp. 66).

Our data set for cluster simplification is a list of Soviet-era loanwords noted in a Russian-language paper by Memetov (1993). These words were transcribed phonetically using the Cyrillic script. Based on the sounds that the Cyrillic letters tend to make, we have attempted to re-transcribe the words into IPA as follows:

(2) Crimean Tatar Russian
облас [obləs] область [obɫəsʲtʲ] ‘oblast’
част [t͡ɕæst] счастье [ɕːæsʲtʲje] ‘happiness’

In Table 2, the Crimean Tatar form [obləs] lacks the palatalized [tʲ] of the original Russian word, suggesting that [tʲ] has been deleted when the coda [sʲtʲ] enters Crimean Tatar. (Note that [sʲ] has also been unpalatalized.) As for [t͡ɕæst] ‘happiness’, it is difficult to tell whether the loanword has undergone cluster simplification or that it has merely deleted the -ье [je] sequence, which is a Russian nominalizing suffix. The reason for this doubt is because Memetov (1993) also mentioned shortened loanwords like прогрессив ‘progressive’ (from Russian прогрессивный ‘progressive’) and колониалъ ‘colonial’ (from Russian колониальный ‘colonial’), both of which lost the adjective suffix -ный of Russian but could still function as adjectives in Crimean Tatar. While this removal of morphological infixes is an intriguing topic to consider in discussions of language contact, it is not relevant to our phonological analysis and will be dismissed. Our conclusion for this section is that there is just one loanword in the data set that provides a clear example of cluster simplification.

3. No change

In some cases, loanwords that violate the phonotactics of the recipient language remain the same. For example, we have previously discussed that native Finnish words do not allow cluster onsets. However, due to close contact with English and Swedish, many words with initial clusters have entered Finnish without getting the cluster onsets modified, such as presidentti ‘president’ and krokotiili ‘crocodile’ (Campbell, 2021, pg. 67). This phenomenon was also noted in Crimean Tatar by Kavitskaya (2010):

(3) Crimean Tatar Russian
gramota gramota ‘reading and writing’
drama drama ‘drama’
klassifikatsija klassifikatsija ‘classification’

Note that the word прогрессив ‘progressive’ in Memetov (1993) shows this phenomenon too, as its пр [pr] onset is still retained. Regardless, the words listed by Kavitskaya (2010) were characterized by the author as “recent but nonetheless assimilated,” presumably assimilated into widespread use while remaining unassimilated into the phonology. It is unclear how recently the words were borrowed, but in his study of Crimean Tatar, Memetov (1993) also recorded Russian words that were borrowed during the Soviet period and remained unchanged in Crimean Tatar. We thus assume that these unchanged loanwords were also borrowed during the Soviet period, either at the same time as the words in the previous data sets or after them.
