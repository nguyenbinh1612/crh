HISTORICAL CONTEXT



It would be a grave omission to discuss Crimean Tatar without mentioning the history of its native speakers. Scholars agree that the ethnogenesis of the Crimean Tatars took place in the fifteenth century, when the various ethnic groups that had already resided in the Crimean peninsula started merging into one cohesive identity under the political dominance of Islam in the Crimean Khanate (Lazzerini, 1985). It is thus unsurprising that a lot of Arabic words entered the Crimean Tatar language at this time (Memetov, 1993). Then in 1783, the Khanate fell to Russia under Catherine the Great, prompting many Crimean Tatars to flee for the Ottoman Empire and many Slavic settlers to move into Crimea, such that by 1917, Crimean Tatars “made up a mere quarter of the population of their historic homeland” (Glyn Williams, 2002).

When the Soviet Union first rose to power, Crimean Tatars—along with other non-Russian ethnic groups—were mainly tolerated under the korenizatsiya (indigenization) policy of the early 1920s. In the Crimean Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic (Crimean ASSR) established in 1921, Crimean Tatar was the primary language of schools, museums, road signs, etc., with many newspapers and journals founded to promote mass literacy (Lazzerini, 1985). All this promotion operated in the constraints of what historian Yuri Slezkine calls “ethnic particularism”: an ethnic group was allowed to solidify and promote their native language and culture as long as these efforts aimed at a deep understanding of Soviet revolutionary ideals. (Slezkine, 1998). Thus, any manifestations of what the USSR perceived as “nationalist deviations” would be stamped down, which led to the killings of Crimean Tatar intelligentsia—many of whom communists— that began in 1928 and only intensified in the 1930s (Lazzerini, 1985).

Then World War Two broke out. During the war, Nazi Germany selected Crimean Tatars out of Soviet POWs to fight on their side, driving a wedge between the Slavic and Crimean Tatar inhabitants of Crimea (Glyn Williams, 2002). As collective punishment for collaborating with the Nazis, between May 18 and May 20, 1944, the Soviet secret police (NKVD) deported approximately 250,000 Crimean Tatars—including members of the Communist Party—to faraway Central Asia (see: Lazzerini, 1985; Bezverkha, 2015). Tens of thousands died, and the Crimean ASSR was abolished soon after. When WWII was over, decorated Red Army veterans of Crimean Tatar descent like Cevdet Dermenci, Abdraim Reshidov and Idris Khaybulaev found themselves forced into exile and not allowed to reenter Crimea. Other veterans of mixed descent like Amet-khan Sultan were caught between two worlds: the NKVD classified them as non-Tatars and let their families reside in Crimea, yet they insisted on identifying as Crimean Tatar and even participated in the Crimean Tatar civil rights movement of the 1950s (Uehling, 2004).

In the following decades, Crimean Tatars fought for the rights to return to their homeland. On September 5, 1967, the Presidium of the Supreme Soviet of the USSR issued a decree that cancelled the Stalin-era accusations of treason against Crimean Tatars. Twenty-two years later, the Soviet government officially permitted Crimean Tatars to return home, albeit with great hostility from many of the now-predominant Russian settlers, who protested by reevoking the accusations of the past (Uehling, 2004). According to Bezverkha (2015), by the time Crimean Tatars were able to resettle in Crimea, they had suffered significant cultural loss, and the Russian language had gained dominance in their homeland. Nowadays, Crimean Tatar is a language with a combination of Arabic, Russian and Uzbek influences, and in many families, the language is not being passed down to children, thus putting it in severe endangerment.
